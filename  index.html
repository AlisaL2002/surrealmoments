Surreal Moments

History

Surrealism originally started as a literary movement in the late 1910s and early '20s. It experimented with a new mode of expression called automatic writing, which aimed to release the uncontrolled imagination of the human subconsciousness. Not too long after, it spread all across Europe and became an international social and political movement. What was so special about the new movement is that it completely disregarded the traditions cultivated a decade earlier by Dadaism.

Surrealism seemed like an intriguing movement to many artists of the 20th century. It was so new and fascinating that it prompted many experiments that affected and explored the human consciousness. Hypnotism was one of those experiments. A group of curious artists, among which were André Breton and Max Ernst, utilized it as a means to access unconscious creativity; however, later decided the experiments were dangerous.

You can’t necessarily define surrealism as abstract or representational art. A surreal image is not exactly what you see on a canvas, but at the same time, it is all the things you see at once. There are no specific rules when it comes to understanding or interpreting it. Surreal artists explore profound personal rather than social topics like the Dada Movement did and the most fascinating parts of our minds, such as consciousness, as well as the world of dreams (inspired by Sigmund Freud’s theories of dreams) and the irrational in their art.

Nowadays, the Surrealism Art Movement's legacy lives on. Many modern museums, including The Metropolitan Museum of Art (MET), The Museum of Modern Art (MoMA), and The Dalí in the United States, display many paintings of such influential artists as Salvador Dali, René Magritte, and Max Ernst. The museums and galleries serve to pass on the legacy and introduce the younger generation to various artistic movements, including Surrealism.
